Patterns
Learned rules extracted from your historical Klaviyo data. Star patterns you want the calendar and copy generators to prioritize.
How to read these patterns
Confidence badges explained
  • High. 50+ supporting campaigns AND a strong effect (open rate delta 3+ pts, CTR 0.5+ pts, or RPR $0.05+). Act on these.
  • Medium. 20 to 49 campaigns OR 50+ with a smaller effect. Worth acting on but validate first.
  • Exploratory. Under 20 campaigns or weak effect. Treat as a hypothesis, not a conclusion.
  • Calibration note. Badges are computed from sample size and effect size, not self-assessed. This prevents the AI from over-claiming on thin data.
Assessing a pattern
  • Trust the numbers, not the narrative. Read the evidence field. If numbers do not support the rule, dismiss it.
  • Gut-check against brand. You know SFH better than the data. A pattern like "use urgency" might conflict with Ines voice. Dismiss when strategy and stats disagree.
  • Check the example winners. If the 3 sample subject lines do not obviously demonstrate the rule, the pattern is likely overreaching.
  • Watch for seasonal or segment bias. BFCM and highly engaged segments skew results. A pattern that leans on these is narrower than it looks.
  • Specific predicted numbers are often decorative. Claude sometimes writes precise-looking forecasts like "lifts opens from 28% to 38%" that sound authoritative but are educated guesses. The direction is usually right; the magnitude is often fabricated.
  • The pragmatic test. Would you confidently act on this in your next send? Yes, star it. No, dismiss it.
When to re-run extraction
  • Monthly, after new Klaviyo uploads. Fresh data can surface new patterns or strengthen existing ones. Make it part of your SFH reporting rhythm.
  • When switching time ranges. All time shows foundational winners. Last 12 months shows current relevance. Last 90 days catches recent shifts.
  • After a big launch or promo. Major campaigns can shift what is working. Re-extract to see what changed.
  • Once is enough for a given range. Patterns save permanently in Supabase. You only re-run to analyze new data or a new time window.
  • Compare All time vs Last 12 months. Patterns that show up in both are durable. Patterns only in one view are worth watching.
  • Duplicates happen. Running extraction twice on the same range can produce similar patterns. Dismiss the dupes manually for now.

Flow Diagnostics
Learned diagnoses for each active flow. Severity tags show what to fix now, grow, monitor, or test.
How to read flow diagnostics
Severity categories
  • Fix now. The flow or message is leaking revenue or reputation. Action this week.
  • Opportunity. Room to grow this flow meaningfully. Schedule in the next sprint.
  • Monitor. Worth watching. Not urgent but keep an eye on the trend.
  • A/B test. A specific hypothesis worth testing. See the test hint inside the card.
Confidence badges explained
  • Message-scope. High needs 5,000+ deliveries. Medium needs 1,000 to 5,000. Below 1,000 is exploratory.
  • Flow-scope. High needs 10,000+ total deliveries. Medium needs 2,000 to 10,000. Below is exploratory.
  • Cross-flow or structural. High needs 15,000+ combined deliveries. Medium needs 3,000 to 15,000.
  • Calibration note. Badges are computed deterministically from sample size, not self-assessed. A thin-sample diagnosis labeled exploratory may still have a correct observation, but the magnitude is not trustworthy.
Status and freshness pills
  • Live. Flow is auto-triggered and actively running.
  • Manual. Flow requires someone to trigger it. Useful for seasonal or campaign-tied flows.
  • Worth revisiting (orange). Flow name or messages suggest a newer version exists. Data may mix old and new. Check with SFH before acting.
Assessing a diagnosis
  • Compare the recommendation to SFH voice. Dismiss if the fix suggests tactics that conflict with brand.
  • A/B test predicted effects are often decorative. "Lifts conversion from 0 to 0.3%" sounds precise but is an educated guess. Trust the direction, treat the magnitude as approximate.
  • Worth-revisiting flows may be mid-migration. Check with the SFH team before restructuring.
  • Structural recommendations need team review. Audits like "archive variants under 500 deliveries" may remove intentional segment-specific variants. Validate before auto-applying.
  • Star what you will act on. Session 3 idea and copy generators will use starred diagnoses as priorities.
When to re-run diagnosis
  • After monthly Klaviyo flow uploads. New data can surface new issues or strengthen existing ones.
  • After you act on a recommendation. Wait for new data to accumulate, re-upload, then re-diagnose to see if the fix worked.
  • After launching a new flow or restructuring. Once the new version has 2 to 3 weeks of data.
  • Once is enough otherwise. Diagnoses save permanently. Re-running on unchanged data produces similar findings.

Content Buckets
The 5 SFH content categories from Ines voice doc. Ideas and Draft generators use these as organizing principles. Edit a bucket to refine how it steers future output.
Voice Brief
SFH voice and strategy reference. Structured fields drive hard constraints in generators. Narrative sections provide context. Edit anything to keep it current.
Loaded
How the Voice Brief is used
What generators read
  • Structured fields on the left. Voice adjectives, signature phrases, banned words, and banned claim types become hard constraints in every prompt. Generators cannot output banned language.
  • Narrative sections on the right. Brand snapshot, customer profile, differentiation, and positioning give Claude context for the deeper "why" behind SFH output.
  • Editing flows through immediately. Changes save on blur. Next Ideas or Draft run picks up the new voice.
When to update
  • Positioning shifts. If SFH launches a new product line or changes market positioning, update the positioning and differentiation sections.
  • New banned language surfaces. Any time you dismiss generated copy because of a word or phrase, add it to banned words so it never comes back.
  • Signature phrases evolve. When a winning phrase emerges from campaigns, add it here so Claude can reach for it.
Products
SFH product catalog. Ideas and Draft generators read from here to avoid inventing product details or claims. Edit each product to refine its description, ingredients, signature claims, and stack pairs.
How Products ground generation
What generators read
  • Short description. The 1-2 sentence pitch. Used when an email features the product.
  • Key ingredients. Keeps Claude from inventing "clinical doses" or ingredient claims.
  • Signature claims. Brand-approved factual phrases. Claude pulls from this list rather than writing its own.
  • Stack pairs. Guides Claude when suggesting routines. Behavioral framing, not biochemical.
  • Notes. Anything worth Claude knowing about how SFH talks about this product.
Tips
  • Refine signature claims from real product pages. The seeded claims are starting points. Paste what your product page actually says.
  • Stack pairs are behavioral. "Creatine + Magnesium" means they fit in the same routine, not that one enhances the other.
  • Notes is where judgment lives. If a product has a specific talking point you dont want generators missing, put it here.
Ideas
Generate email concepts grounded in SFH voice, your starred patterns, and your starred flow diagnostics. Ideas become the backbone of your calendar and the starting point for drafts.
How Ideas generation works
What Claude sees when generating
  • Selected bucket definition. Name, description, key patterns, hook examples, and "what makes it good" from the Buckets tab.
  • Your starred patterns. Every pattern you starred under Patterns. If you have none, Claude uses your active patterns.
  • Your starred flow diagnostics. Starred flow findings so ideas can address known gaps and losers.
  • Voice brief structured fields. Banned words, banned claim types, signature phrases, POV, formality all become hard constraints.
  • Voice brief narrative sections. Brand snapshot, customer profile, differentiation, positioning as context.
  • Optional extra context. Anything you add in the form (seasonality, launch, campaign focus).
Assessing an idea
  • Check the hook. Would you actually want to open this email? If not, dismiss.
  • Check the grounded-in tags. Every idea cites which starred pattern or diagnostic shaped it. If the attribution feels thin or wrong, the idea is probably off.
  • Star what you want to build. Starred ideas feed Draft generation next.
  • Promote to calendar. Click "Add to Waiting Bay" to schedule the concept for a real send.
If generation feels repetitive
  • Star patterns more aggressively. More starred intel gives Claude more distinct angles to draw from.
  • Use extra context. "Creatine launch in 2 weeks" or "Coming off BFCM, focus on new-year health reset" pushes generation toward fresh territory.
  • Dismiss aggressively. Dismissed ideas arent shown to Claude on next run, so you wont get the same idea twice.

Draft
Generate full email drafts from a starred idea. Ines structure: headline, subheadline, first CTA, body, bridge, product section, final CTA. Two variants per draft for A/B testing.
How Draft generation works
What Claude sees when generating
  • The source idea. Hook, angle, subject options, preview, CTA angle, reasoning. The draft stays faithful to the idea.
  • The bucket definition. Keeps the draft shaped to the bucket format.
  • Voice brief. Structured fields are hard constraints. Narrative fields are context.
  • Top winning subject lines. The best historical subjects from your Klaviyo data serve as voice anchors.
  • Optional extra direction. Anything you add in the form (product focus, specific angle refinement, length tweaks).
Variant strategies
  • Different angle. A and B explore different angles of the same idea. Better for testing which hook framing resonates. More divergent, requires judgment on which lands.
  • Tonal variant. A is the main take. B is the same angle executed with a different tone or emphasis. Better for subject-line A/B testing while keeping the core message consistent.
Working with drafts
  • Pick a preferred variant. Click the A or B radio to mark which you prefer. This feeds future copy generation.
  • Star what is close. Starred drafts surface in the Campaign Editor when we build it next session.
  • Dismiss to regenerate. The idea stays starred. You can generate a fresh draft from the same idea anytime.
  • Add to Waiting Bay creates a calendar row with the preferred variant prefilled.
